# Dodecanese Sailing Route: Athens to Rhodes
## April 18 -- May 2, 2026 (14 nights / 15 days)

---

## 1. Day-by-Day Itinerary

The challenge with this route is that Athens (Alimos Marina or Lavrion) is approximately 130-180 nautical miles from Patmos, your first Dodecanese stop. Getting to Patmos efficiently requires either transit days through the Cyclades, or a repositioning strategy. Below is the recommended itinerary that balances transit sailing with island time.

### PHASE 1: Athens to Patmos (Transit, 3 days)

**Day 1 (Apr 18) -- Athens/Lavrion to Kea (Tzia)**
- Distance: ~14 NM from Lavrion
- Sailing time: 2-3 hours
- Depart Lavrion mid-morning after charter check-in. Short, easy first sail.
- Anchorage: Korissia harbor or Vourkari bay (well-protected, tavernas ashore)
- This is a gentle warm-up sail.

**Day 2 (Apr 19) -- Kea to Mykonos (via Syros optional stop)**
- Distance: ~55 NM (direct Kea to Mykonos) or split: Kea to Syros ~40 NM, Syros to Mykonos ~20 NM
- Sailing time: 8-10 hours (direct) or split into two legs
- Option A: Long day sail direct to Mykonos
- Option B: Stop in Syros (Ermoupoli) for lunch, continue to Mykonos
- Anchorage: Ornos Bay or Mykonos old port

**Day 3 (Apr 20) -- Mykonos to Patmos**
- Distance: ~65 NM
- Sailing time: 10-12 hours (early start required, ~0600)
- This is the big transit day. Leave at dawn, arrive late afternoon.
- Alternative: Break this with an overnight in Ikaria or Fourni if weather demands it.

### PHASE 2: The Dodecanese, North to South (11 days)

**Day 4 (Apr 21) -- Patmos (rest day / exploration)**
- Distance: 0 NM (stay put)
- Anchorage: Skala Port (main harbor, water/fuel/provisions available)
- **Key attractions:**
  - Monastery of Saint John the Theologian (UNESCO World Heritage, founded 1088)
  - Cave of the Apocalypse (UNESCO) -- where Saint John dictated the Book of Revelation to his disciple Prochoros in ~95 AD. The cave still shows the three cracks in the rock said to represent the Holy Trinity, and the stone ledge where John rested his head.
  - Chora -- the hilltop capital with labyrinthine whitewashed streets, artisan shops, and sweeping views
  - Beaches: Psili Ammos (best beach, boat-access recommended), Lambi (colorful volcanic pebbles), Livadi Geranou
- **Restaurants:** Waterfront tavernas in Skala serving fresh seafood, grilled octopus, and local "pougia" cheese pies. Try Benetos for upscale Mediterranean dining in a garden setting.

**Day 5 (Apr 22) -- Patmos to Lipsi**
- Distance: ~12 NM
- Sailing time: 2-3 hours
- A short, relaxed morning sail to this tiny, unspoiled gem.
- Anchorage: Lipsi main harbor (sheltered pier) or Katsadia bay (popular anchorage for yachts)
- **Key attractions:**
  - Koimisis tis Theokou -- Byzantine church (7th-8th century) with a revered icon of the Virgin
  - Lipsi Winery -- wine tastings on Fridays at 19:00 (15 EUR)
  - Beaches: Hohlakoura, Platis Gialos, Monodendri
  - Boat trips to Tiganakia lagoon and surrounding uninhabited islets
  - Hiking -- most places reachable within 30-45 minutes on foot
- **Restaurants:**
  - Kalypso -- harbor-front, locally sourced seafood
  - Manolis Tastes -- family-run, seafood and grilled meats
  - Pefko -- genuine local gastronomy, fish and fresh seafood
  - Sofoklis / Asprakis -- traditional ouzeries with small plates

**Day 6 (Apr 23) -- Lipsi to Leros**
- Distance: ~10 NM
- Sailing time: 1.5-2 hours
- Another short sail, leaving time for thorough exploration.
- Anchorage: Lakki (one of the Mediterranean's finest natural bays -- enormous, deep, perfectly protected) or Panteli fishing harbor (charming, fish tavernas right on the beach)
- **Key attractions:**
  - Lakki -- remarkable Art Deco town built during Italian occupation (1920s-40s), unique in Greece
  - Castle of Leros (Panagia) -- 360-degree views, spectacular sunsets
  - Agios Isidoros -- picturesque 14th-century church on a rocky islet, connected to shore by a thin walkway over the water
  - Six Windmills above Panteli village
  - Beaches: Vromolithos, Blefoutis, Alinda, Gourna
  - Boatyards at Partheni Bay (Artemis and Dock & Moore) for any repairs needed
- **Restaurants:**
  - Mylos -- exceptional food and amazing views
  - Zorbas -- no-frills honest home cooking, let them decide your meal
  - Fish tavernas lining Panteli beach

**Day 7 (Apr 24) -- Leros to Kalymnos**
- Distance: ~12 NM
- Sailing time: 2-3 hours
- Anchorage: Pothia harbor (the capital, full of color and energy) or Vathys fjord (spectacular narrow inlet, very protected)
- **Key attractions:**
  - World-class rock climbing -- 2,700+ equipped routes in 50+ crags (limestone cliffs, tufa formations)
  - Sponge diving heritage -- once the world's largest sponge fleet. Visit the Naval Museum in Pothia for the documentary about divers' dangerous profession
  - Pothia waterfront -- colorful neoclassical houses, lively fishing port
  - Massouri/Myrties -- tourist center on the west coast with views to Telendos islet
  - Telendos islet -- a 10-minute water taxi ride, peaceful and undeveloped
- **Restaurants:**
  - Harry's Paradise -- widely considered the island's best
  - Aegean Tavern -- traditional seafood
  - Prego -- pan-Mediterranean cuisine
  - Pothia waterfront tavernas -- fresh seafood and ouzo

**Day 8 (Apr 25) -- Kalymnos to Kos**
- Distance: ~8 NM (via Pserimos) or ~12 NM direct
- Sailing time: 1.5-2 hours
- Option: Stop at tiny Pserimos island (2.5 NM from Kalymnos, 2 NM from Kos) for a swim in turquoise waters before continuing to Kos.
- Anchorage: Kos Marina (full-service marina, well-protected)
- **Key attractions:**
  - Asklepieion -- the ancient healing center following Hippocrates' medical guidelines, 4 km from Kos Town. One of the most important archaeological sites in the Dodecanese.
  - Kos Town -- Neratzia Medieval Castle (Knights of St. John), ancient Greek Agora, Roman Odeon
  - Plane Tree of Hippocrates -- where Hippocrates supposedly taught his students
  - Zia village -- mountain village 12 km inland, famous sunset views
  - Beaches: Marmari to Mastichari (long dune-like stretches), Therma (natural hot springs in the sea)
  - Cycling -- Kos is flat and bike-friendly, the best way to explore
- **Restaurants:**
  - Karnagio Taverna (Zipari) -- family-run below the Asklepieion, seaside, home cooking
  - Makis (Mastichari) -- incredible prices, genuine Greek experience
  - Arap (Platani) -- authentic Turkish food, reflecting Kos's proximity to Turkey
  - Agios Theologos -- sea views, Greek appetizers, sunset views

**Day 9 (Apr 26) -- Kos (rest day / exploration)**
- Distance: 0 NM
- Full day to explore the Asklepieion in the morning (less crowded), cycle around the island, visit Zia village for sunset. Resupply provisions.

**Day 10 (Apr 27) -- Kos to Nisyros**
- Distance: ~18 NM
- Sailing time: 3-4 hours
- Anchorage: Pali harbor (northeast corner, dredged and enlarged, good shelter from northerlies, 3-4m depth, water and electricity) or Mandraki (main town harbor)
- **Key attractions:**
  - Stefanos Crater -- walk inside an active volcanic caldera almost 4 km wide. Fumaroles hiss, sulfur crystals stain rocks yellow, ground radiates heat. Entrance fee: 5 EUR.
  - Polyvotis Crater -- lesser-known twin craters, fewer crowds
  - Mandraki -- sugar-cubed houses in amphitheatre shape, medieval castle above
  - Panagia Spiliani -- cliff-side monastery
  - Volcanological Museum in Nikia -- the only museum in Greece dedicated entirely to volcanic and seismic history
  - Nikia village -- whitewashed houses, incredible viewpoints directly into the crater
  - Hot springs -- mineral-rich thermal waters
- **Restaurants:**
  - Gevsea (Mandraki) -- family-run, fantastic fish and Greek fusion
  - The Balcony of Emporeios -- volcano views, lunch and dinner overlooking the crater
  - Port tavernas in Pali -- freshly caught fish, typical Greek cuisine

**Day 11 (Apr 28) -- Nisyros to Symi**
- Distance: ~35 NM (this is the longest Dodecanese leg)
- Sailing time: 6-7 hours
- An early-ish start recommended. Beautiful sailing past the Turkish coast.
- Anchorage: Gialos harbor (Symi town) -- moor stern-to in 15-20m if space available. Deep harbor, mud/rock bottom. Alternatively, anchor in one of the smaller bays: Nanou, Marathounda, or Ag. Emilianos.
- **Key attractions:**
  - Gialos Harbor -- the iconic photograph. Hundreds of neoclassical mansions in bright pastel tones climbing the hillsides, reflected in azure waters. Built 1860-1910 from sponge diving wealth, inspired by Smyrna architecture.
  - Chorio -- the upper town, connected by 500 steps (the Kali Strata). Castle ruins and windmills at the top.
  - Sponge diving heritage -- Symi once had the world's largest sponge fleet. Divers free-dove 60+ meters naked with 20kg stones tied to their bodies. Fotis Mastoridis brought the first autonomous diving suit in 1860.
  - Neoclassical facades -- pediments, mouldings, decorative pillars, painted window frames. Each house unique.
  - Symi's famous tiny shrimp -- a local delicacy you must try
- **Restaurants:**
  - Harborside fish restaurants -- widely considered to serve the best seafood in the Dodecanese. Mussels steamed in white wine, mixed grills of octopus/squid/prawns over wood fire.
  - Tavernas along the waterfront in Gialos

**Day 12 (Apr 29) -- Symi exploration + Panormitis**
- Distance: ~11 NM (Symi town to Panormitis and back, or anchor overnight at Panormitis)
- Sailing time: 2 hours
- Sail around to the southwest side to visit:
  - **Monastery of Archangel Michael at Panormitis** -- 18th-century seaside monastery with the world's largest baroque-style bell tower. Two museums and a library with Byzantine manuscripts. Marvelous Byzantine frescoes and wood-carved icon screen. People of all faiths visit to pray for health and protection.
  - Swimming in the bay of Panormitis
  - Hike the coastal paths
- Return to Symi town or anchor at Panormitis overnight (peaceful).

**Day 13 (Apr 30) -- Symi to Rhodes**
- Distance: ~25 NM
- Sailing time: 4-5 hours
- Morning departure, arrive Rhodes early afternoon.
- Anchorage: Mandraki Harbor (the historic harbor where the Colossus of Rhodes once stood). Pleasure craft and yachts berth here. Alternatively, Rhodes Marina for full-service facilities.
- **Key attractions:**
  - Rhodes Medieval Old Town (UNESCO World Heritage) -- one of the best-preserved walled medieval cities in the world, built by the Knights of St. John in the 14th century.
  - Palace of the Grand Master -- Gothic castle, originally a 7th-century Byzantine citadel, converted by the Knights Hospitaller after 1309
  - Street of the Knights -- principal east-west axis with seven preserved inns representing the Knights' homelands (England, France, Germany, Italy, Provence, Auvergne, Aragon). One of the finest Gothic urban ensembles in the world.
  - Mandraki Harbor -- bronze deer statues (Elefos and Elafina) at the entrance, 15th-century Fortress of Agios Nikolaos with lighthouse, medieval windmills
  - Church of the Annunciation (Rhodes Cathedral) -- built 1925, Italian architecture with Byzantine frescoes
  - Nea Agora (New Market) -- 1920s market with Ottoman-influenced architecture
  - Lindos (45 min drive) -- ancient acropolis perched above a stunning bay
- **Restaurants:**
  - Alexis 4 Seasons -- inside medieval walls, rooftop terrace with ancient city views, Mediterranean cuisine with contemporary flair
  - Kerasma -- near Mandraki, excellent modern Greek
  - Old Town tavernas throughout the medieval quarter

**Day 14 (May 1) -- Rhodes exploration day**
- Distance: 0 NM
- Full day to explore the medieval old town properly: Palace of the Grand Master, Street of the Knights, Jewish Quarter, Ottoman mosques, the old moat gardens. Optional day trip to Lindos.

**Day 15 (May 2) -- Charter return / departure**
- Return yacht at Rhodes marina. Flight home.

## 3. One-Way Charter Fees and Logistics

### Charter Costs (Bareboat, April 2026)

April is shoulder/low season in Greece, which means significantly lower prices than June-August.

| Boat Type | Weekly Rate (April) | 2-Week Estimate |
|-----------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Monohull 40-42 ft | 2,000-3,500 EUR/week | 4,000-7,000 EUR |
| Catamaran 40-42 ft (e.g. Lagoon 40) | 3,000-5,000 EUR/week | 6,000-10,000 EUR |
| Catamaran 45-48 ft (e.g. Lagoon 46) | 4,500-7,000 EUR/week | 9,000-14,000 EUR |

### One-Way Surcharge

A one-way charter (not returning the boat to the base) incurs a **repositioning fee** of approximately **800-1,500 EUR** or more, depending on distance. Athens to Rhodes is a long repositioning, so expect the higher end or even **1,500-2,500 EUR**. Some companies occasionally offer one-way specials with no surcharge when they need boats repositioned.

### Key Charter Companies with Bases in Both Athens and Rhodes/Kos

- **Istion Yachting** -- bases in Athens (Alimos), Kos, and Rhodes. They regularly offer one-way specials. Contact: +30 210 9811 515 / charters@istion.com
- **Sunsail** -- base in Athens/Lavrion
- **Dream Yacht Charter** -- delivery charter specials (one-way offers)
- **Navigare Yachting** -- Athens catamaran base

### Additional Costs to Budget

- Security deposit: 2,000-4,000 EUR (refundable, or buy damage waiver ~200-300 EUR/week)
- Mandatory extras: end cleaning ~200 EUR, outboard motor ~80 EUR/week
- Fuel: ~200-400 EUR for 2 weeks (sailing yacht)
- Provisioning: varies
- Marina/port fees: generally modest in the Dodecanese, ~10-30 EUR/night

### Getting Home from Rhodes

- **Flights Rhodes (RHO) to Athens (ATH):** ~59 minutes. Operated by Aegean Airlines and Sky Express. Approximately 43 flights per week (every ~4 hours). Prices from ~40-90 EUR one-way.
- **Direct international flights from Rhodes:** In late April/early May, some European routes may already operate (UK, Germany, Scandinavia). Check availability closer to date.

## 4. Weather in the Dodecanese -- Late April

Late April is an **excellent** time to sail the Dodecanese. You are after the winter storms but before the summer Meltemi winds.

| Parameter | Late April |
|-----------|-----------|
| Air temp (high) | 19-22 C |
| Air temp (low) | 12-14 C |
| Sea temperature | 17-18 C (swimmable for hardy souls; wetsuits for extended swimming) |
| Rainfall | ~40 mm total for the month, ~5 rain days |
| Sunshine | ~8 hours/day |
| Wind | Light to moderate, 2-4 Beaufort (4-16 knots). Predominantly from SE/S in spring. Occasional calm days. |
| Meltemi | Not yet active (starts slowly in June, peaks July-August) |
| Sea state | Generally calm. No significant swell patterns. |

**Key advantages of late April:**
- Pre-Meltemi: no strong northerlies that make summer Aegean sailing challenging
- Minimal crowds -- marinas and anchorages will be uncrowded
- Lower charter prices (shoulder season)
- Comfortable temperatures for hiking and sightseeing
- Wildflowers in bloom across the islands
- Orthodox Easter sometimes falls in April (2026: April 12, so just before your trip)

**Key considerations:**
- Sea temperature is still cool -- swimming is refreshing rather than warm
- Some seasonal restaurants/bars may not have opened yet on smaller islands (Lipsi, Nisyros)
- Occasional unsettled weather possible -- have 1-2 buffer days in your plan (you do)

## 5. Cultural Highlights in Depth

### Patmos -- Island of the Apocalypse

Patmos is one of the most spiritually significant places in all of Christianity. In approximately 95 AD, Saint John the Theologian (identified by the Eastern Orthodox Church with the Apostle John and the Evangelist) was exiled here during Roman persecution under Emperor Domitian. In a cave halfway up the mountain, he received a series of prophetic visions and dictated the Book of Revelation (the Apocalypse) to his disciple Prochoros.

The cave still preserves:
- Three cracks in the rock, said to symbolize the Holy Trinity
- The stone ledge where John rested his head
- A natural rock shelf used as a writing desk

The Monastery of Saint John the Theologian, founded in 1088, crowns the island. It has been a pilgrimage site and center of Greek Orthodox learning for nearly a millennium. Together with the Cave and the medieval settlement of Chora, it was inscribed as a **UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1999**. Few places in the world maintain religious ceremonies virtually unchanged since early Christian times.

### Rhodes -- Medieval Masterpiece

The Medieval City of Rhodes is a **UNESCO World Heritage Site** and one of the best-preserved walled medieval cities in Europe. After the Knights Hospitaller (Knights of St. John) conquered Rhodes in 1309, they transformed it into their headquarters, building:

- **Palace of the Grand Master** -- a Gothic castle originally built as a 7th-century Byzantine citadel, expanded massively by the Knights. Reconstructed by Italian architects 1937-1940. Contains excellent mosaic floors from Kos.
- **Street of the Knights** -- perhaps the finest surviving example of Gothic urban planning. Seven "inns" (auberges) representing the Knights' nationalities line both sides: England, France, Germany, Italy, Provence, Auvergne, and Aragon.
- **The fortifications** -- 4 km of walls with towers and gates, surrounded by a dry moat
- **Ottoman Quarter** -- after the Ottoman conquest in 1522, mosques, hammams, and fountains were added
- **Jewish Quarter** -- with the Kahal Shalom Synagogue (1577), the oldest in Greece

The blend of Gothic, Byzantine, and Ottoman architecture in one walkable medieval city is unique in the Mediterranean.

### Symi -- Painted Paradise

Symi's extraordinary beauty comes from its history as the wealthiest sponge-diving island in the Aegean. Between 1860 and 1910, profits from sponge trade funded the construction of hundreds of neoclassical mansions painted in bright pastels -- ochre, terracotta, dusty rose, sky blue -- climbing the steep hillsides around Gialos harbor.

The island's sponge divers were legendary: before diving equipment, they free-dove naked to 60+ meters, tying 20kg stones to their bodies for speed. Fotis Mastoridis brought the first autonomous diving suit in 1860, transforming the industry. At its peak, Symi had the world's largest sponge fleet.

The Monastery of Archangel Michael at Panormitis, on the island's southwestern shore, features the world's largest baroque-style bell tower, Byzantine manuscripts, and is a major pilgrimage site where people of all faiths pray for healing and protection.

## 7. Is 2 Weeks Enough? Or Too Rushed?

**Two weeks is a good fit for this route, but with a caveat.**

The transit from Athens to Patmos (3 days, ~134 NM) is the main time cost. Once in the Dodecanese, the islands are close together (8-35 NM between stops), and 11 days to cover 8 islands plus rest days is comfortable.

**What makes it work:**
- 3 rest/exploration days built in (Patmos, Kos, Rhodes)
- Most Dodecanese sailing days are 2-4 hours, leaving afternoons free
- The longest single leg (Nisyros to Symi, ~35 NM) is still only 6-7 hours
- April weather is mild and predictable -- fewer weather delays
- Low season means no fights for anchorage spots

**What could make it feel rushed:**
- The 3 transit days through the Cyclades are pure transport -- if weather forces a delay, it compresses Dodecanese time
- If you want to deeply explore Rhodes (Medieval Town + Lindos + Valley of the Butterflies), one day may not be enough
- Arki archipelago (between Patmos and Lipsi) is stunning and worth a detour, but adds a day

**Alternatives to consider:**
1. **Fly Athens to Kos, charter from Kos** -- eliminates the 3-day transit entirely. Many charter companies have Kos bases. You get 14 full days in the Dodecanese, can add Arki, Agathonisi, Tilos, and extra days in Rhodes. This is the most relaxed option.
2. **Fly to Kos, sail Kos to Rhodes one-way (1 week) + Rhodes to Patmos and back (1 week)** -- Istion Yachting has bases in both Kos and Rhodes.
3. **Keep the Athens start but add Ikaria/Fourni** as transit stops on Day 3, making the crossing more interesting.

**Verdict:** 2 weeks works well. You will not feel rushed in the Dodecanese. The only pressure point is the Athens-to-Patmos transit. If you can fly to Kos instead, the trip becomes downright leisurely and you can add 2-3 more islands.
